A Data-Driven Look at Education and Satisfaction with Life

Introduction

Pursuing Higher Education is a choice that can have a great impact on the course of one's life. To determine if higher education is worthwhile, one would like to know if more education would lead to a greater satisfaction with life. Here, we will explore how higher levels of education correlate with higher levels of satisfaction. We will use the Satisfaction with Life (SWL) Index developed in 1985 and published to the Journal of Personality Assessment along with a large dataset of more than 13,000 individuals. Our results will show that higher levels of education generally do not correspond with higher SWL levels. As such, we suggest that people look away from academics alone when finding pathways to future satisfaction.

Confounders

There are several confounding variables that have to be considered when looking at this data. First, there is the individuals character. Some people may be more happy pursuing higher levels of education than others. As such, their satisfaction will be higher in the process. Similarly, cultural values could play a similarl role. However, due to the randomized nature of the data we consider these confounding variables void. However, an important confounder that we will have to consider is age. Since this data set is drawn from students, there large number of younger age samples as opposed to older ones. This means that any data we gain can give us insight to the age group we have substaintial data on, but may not give us information beyond that. In addition, analysis will likely not tell us how having higher level degrees for many years influences your occupation, in turn influencing your satisfaction with life.

Colliders

There are three major colliders we can consider. First is occupation. Some jobs advocate for or expect people to obtain or already have higher level degrees. In addition, occupation is likely highly influenced by an individuals satisfaction with life. Secondly, we can look at friendships an individal has. Having higher level degrees exposes you to different people, potentially changing your crowd. Beyond this, your satisfaction with life could influence who you befriend as people band together with those similar to them. Finally, one could look at Location, which is heavily influenced by degree level as certain areas may not have nearby schools. In addition, people who happy in life would likely pick to live might live in different places than those unhappy with life. However, our data set does not contain information relating to any of these colliders. As such, we cannot explicitly account for them in our analysis, so we will consider the randomized nature of the data to void any colliders.

Satisfaction

In this section, we will look at how degree level infleunces satisfaction with life. To do this, we will use the Satisfaction with Life (SWL) Index estbalished in 1985. This scale is a self-rating scale where an individual answer 5 questions, each on a scale from 1 to 7. Once answered, the results are summed and a total score is given. Below are the values that give breakpoints for different classifications:

  • 31 - 35: Extremely satisfied
  • 26 - 30: Satisfied
  • 21 - 25: Slightly satisfied
  • 20: Neutral
  • 15 - 19: Slightly dissatisfied
  • 10 - 14: Dissatisfied
  • 5 - 9: Extremely dissatisfied
In the below figure, one can see the relationship between degree level and SWL Index.

From this figure, we can see that although the average values are very close, there is a slight positive trend between education level and SWL. Those who answered None or High school were slightly below 20, demonstrating they are slightly dissatisfied on this scale. On the other hand, those with Bachelor's degrees showed just above 20, in the slightly satisfied territory. There continues to be an upward trend with individuals with Masters having an average around 23 while those with terminal degrees average at around 24. Although all of these points are close to the Neutral territory, there is a slight positive trend.

Although the positive trend is present, since we have not yet dealt with all colliders and confounders in detail we cannot definitively say that higher levels of education lead to higher levels of Satisfaction. However, the research done here has shown there is promising potential for further research in this topic.
