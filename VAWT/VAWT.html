Vertical Axis Wind Turbine (VAWT)

Introduction

As a part of our Junior Lab course, we were asked to design and build a Vertical Axis Wind Turbine (VAWT). To do this, we began by doing research on the different types of VAWTS; Drag-Driven and Lift-Driven. Each has their own benefits and restrictions. For example, Drag-Driven VAWTs are generally self starting, meaning they do not need to be initially moving to generate power. They are also much easier to build. On the other hand, Lift-Driven VAWTs have a much higher efficiency but come with manufacturing difficulties as airfoil geometry can be difficult to create, especially given our manufacturing processes (by hand).

Process and Results

To begin the design process, we started with brain storming several ideas. Drag based, Lift Based, Hybrid, and anything we could think of. We settled on 3 major designs, one drag, one lift, and one hybrid. From here, we designed and 3D printed scale models of these designs. This was used with our in house wind tunnel to do scale model testing. This allowed us to apply concepts we had learned from dimensional analysis to anlayze wind turbines. We were told that Reynolds Number and Tip Speed Ratio were two very important factors for efficiency, so we had to ensure that we kep this in mind when testing.

Once we had the data, we analyzed it to see which design would have a good balance of manufacturability and power generation. We settled on the drag based design, since it provided similar power output to the lift and hybrid designs while being much easier to manufacture. Once we settled on this design, we scaled it up while maintaining dimensional similarity. As can be seen below, we first made a laser cut skeleton to provide support to the sheets we would use to make the turbine surface. We then worked together to apply adhesive evenly and careful to hold the sheets to the skeleton as smoothly as possible.

As can be seen from the completed turbine, the completed design was stable. We tested the VAWT by attaching it to a cart and running with it in a sort of power competition. Our Turbine had the ability to generate just under 1 Watt of power when moving at about running speed. Although this was not enough to win the power competition, we were happy that it produced energy.

This project was one of my personal favorite labs as it gave us experience combining many facets of engineering. From testing in the wind tunnel, to creating a model to see how much we had to scale up the turbine, to manufacturing, I felt like everything had some thought spent on it. This type of engineering makes sure that I keep using knowledge that I otherwise might not have the chance to use.
